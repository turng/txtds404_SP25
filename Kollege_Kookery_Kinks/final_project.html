Editorial Introduction

Kollege Kookery Kinks is a quirky, playful cookbook produced by the Young Women’s Christian Association (YWCA) at the University of Washington (UW), published in 1920. It provides an array of recipes with practical tips and instructions for simple meals. This text was initially meant for the audience of young, collegiate women wanting to learn simple, easy recipes that require no prior cooking experience. This original audience reflects young women's domestic and social lives while blending with the trend of the rise of women in higher education. This cookbook provides simple recipes, but is also a cultural artifact representing the social lives, diets, and collective community of UW women in the early 1900s.

Our edition of Kollege Kookery Kinks aims to provide readers with a more accessible format by digitizing the text for modern readers. We prioritized preserving the original text’s historical value and authenticity. Therefore, the audience of our edition should be able to glimpse what the early 20th-century cooking methods and diets were like. Readers can analyze and compare these recipes compiled in the 1920s to modern-day recipes and diets. The readers of our edition will primarily target contemporary UW students and can extend, but not limit to, researchers and UW alumni. We hope that making this text digitally accessible broadens the outreach to people outside the limited few who have access to UW’s Special Collections Library.

Editorial choices that we’ve made were made with the intent of honoring the cookbook’s historical authenticity. We’ve retained the original recipes' spelling, grammar, format, graphics, and additional quotes. Annotations added to the footnotes provide further explanations of outdated terms or references. The sections we’ve selected to transcribe are “Dainty Desserts” and “Cakes”. This was chosen out of the appeal for the recipes themselves, and to align with the playful tone of the cookbook. Our edition of Kollege Kookery Kinks provides an authentic transcription, making this resource more accessible and user-friendly.

DATE PUDDING

  • 1 cup dates
  • 1 cup nuts
  • ¾ cup sugar
  • 6 level tablespoons flour
  • 2 level teaspoons baking powder
  • ½ cup milk

Bake in moderate oven. Serve with whipped cream.

Gertrude Elliott

MARSHMALLOW DELIGHT

  • 1 lb. best marshmallows
  • 60 maraschino cherries
  • 2. cups chopped nuts
  • 1 pint whipping cream
  • Enough sugar to sweeten

Beat cream until thick. Cut marshmallows into small pieces with scissors and add to cream. Add cherries and nuts and all juice of cherries. Sweeten to taste.

Mrs. J. Allen Smith.

STUFFED APPLES

Hollow out the cores of good baking apples and stuff with a mixture of chopped dates, nuts and figs, and then bake them. Serve with whipped cream and a big cherry on top of each. These make a most delicious dessert served with lady fingers.

Helen Spaulding.

MAPLE PARFAIT

  • 1 cup maple sugar
  • Yolk of 6 eggs
  • 1 pint of cream

Heat syrup to boiling point; pour into beaten egg yolks and the whipped cream; turn into mould and freeze. Serve with chopped brown almonds.

Mrs. Wingate.

FRESH FRUIT MOUSSE

Crush a dozen and a half ripe peaches and sweeten. Whip one quart of cream and add to the fruit. Flavor with vanilla. Put the mixture in a mould and pack with cracked ice and salt. Change the packing three times and at the end of four hours the mousse will be frozen. Any kind of fresh fruit may be substituted.

Elizabeth Daulton.

AMBROSIA

Slice oranges and bananas, sprinkle with cocoanut, squeeze lemon juice over it, serve with whipped cream.

Alice Smith.

BRANBERRY TARTS

  • 1 cup raisins, ground fine
  • 1 milk cracker, ground fine
  • 1 lemon, juice and peeling
  • 1 cup sugar
  • Butter size of an egg
  • 2 tablespoons water
  • Nutmeg and cinnamon to taste

Boil and put into crust.

Ruth Pepper.

CHOCOLATE PUFFS

Beat white of egg until very light; add confectioner's sugar and cocoa until a good paste. Add pinch of salt and vanilla to taste. Mould into little balls, roll in granulated sugar and place in oven for three minutes.

CARAMEL PUDDING

  • 2 cups brown sugar
  • 2 cups water
  • 2 rounding tsp. cornstarch
  • 2 tsp. vanilla
  • ½ cup nut meats

Dissolve sugar in water and bring to a boil. Add cornstarch dissolved in a little water; boil three minutes (or until clear). Remove from fire, add vanilla and nuts and put in moulds until firm. Serve with whipped cream.

Blanche Troutman

ANGELS DELIGHT

  • 1 can pineapple
  • ½ lb. marshmallows
  • 1 cup chopped nuts
  • 1 cup chopped dates

Cut marshmallows in small pieces and soak in pineapple juice for three or four hours. Cut pineapple in small pieces, mix marshmallows, pineapple, nuts and dates together. Serve with whipped cream.

Lillian Everett.

FUDGE PUDDING

  • 1 cup sugar
  • ½ cup molasses
  • ¼ cup hot water
  • ½ cup sour milk
  • 3 sq. melted chocolate
  • 2 egg yolks
  • ½ cup butter
  • 1 tsp. Soda

Make a batter of these ingredients and bake in hot buttered muffin pans. Serve hot with whipped cream. If cream is not available serve milk sauce with individual moulds. Makes 1½ dozens.

BLACK PUDDING

  • 1 tbsp. butter
  • Pinch salt
  • 1 cup flour
  • 1tbsp. butter

Mix ½ cup molasses and ½ cup sweet milk. Steam 1 or 2 hours. Serve with orange sauce.

  • 1 tbsp. butter
  • 2 tbsp. flour
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 1 cup boiling water
  • 1 cup cream
  • Juice of 1 orange

BROWNIES

Melt 2 squares chocolate and ½ cup butter. Add 2 beaten eggs, 1 cup sugar, little vanilla and ½ cup flour, 1 pound of nuts. Spread as you do ginger bread and bake for 30 minutes. When cool cut in squares.

M. Southard.

CREAM PUFFS

  • 1 cup flour
  • ½ cup butter
  • 1 cup boiling water
  • 2 eggs

Put the cup of sifted flour in the boiling water and butter. Stir briskly and take off the stove. Mix in two eggs well beaten. Stir this until it is smooth. Put in buttered muffin pans; about two tablespoons batter to one puff. Bake in a moderate oven. Fill with whipped cream, sweetened or cream custard.

Esther R. Palmer.

PINEAPPLE COMPOTE

  • 1 cup rice
  • 1 teaspoon butter
  • Lemon juice
  • Sugar
  • Slice of pineapple

Boil the rice quickly in plenty of wa-ter, drain in a sieve and dry on back of stove. Form hot rice into moulds about size of pineapples and place pineapple on top of each. Make a sauce by adding the butter; sugar to taste, and a little lemon juice to the pineapple juice. Pour over rice and pineapple. Serve hot.

SPANISH CREAM

  • 1 quart scalded milk
  • ½ package gelatine
  • 6 eggs
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla

Dissolve gelatine in cold water and add to scalded milk. Beat egg yolks, add sugar, stir into milk and cook like custard. When done stir in stiffly beaten whites and add vanilla. Pour into moulds.

MERINGUES

  • Whites of 2 eggs
  • ½ cup sugar

Beat whites until very stiff and dry. Beat in sugar. Drop on buttered tins and bake in oven which is only slightly warm for 1¼ hours. The baking is very important. They should rise and then brown. Cut tops and fill with whipped cream and sliced bananas or fruit in season.

Roszelle Milburn.

FRUIT SHERBET

Three cups water; 2½ cups sugar.Boil water and sugar for 15 minutes and cool.

  • 3 lemons
  • 3 oranges
  • 3 bananas
  • ½ cup apricots

Rub fruits through sieve and freeze.

CUSTARD SOUFFLE (Fruit Sauce)

  • 3 teaspoons butter
  • 3 teaspoons flour
  • 1 cup milk
  • ¼ cup sugar
  • 4 eggs
  • ⅛ teaspoon salt
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla

Melt butter and add flour and salt. Pour in milk and stir until it thickens. Cool, then add sugar and yolks well beaten. Add vanilla and lastly whites beaten stiff and dry. Butter pan and if oven is too hot put souffle in pan of warm water. Never let water boil in pan, but add a little cold water if necessary. Bake 30 minutes in moderate oven.

JAM CAKE

  • 3 eggs

  • ¾ cup butter
  • 1½ cups flour
  • 1 cup jam
  • 3 tablespoons sour cream
  • 1 teaspoonful soda
  • Spices of all kinds
Alice Smith

COCOANUT DROPS

  • 1 cup sugar
  • 1–3 cup hot water

Boil sugar and water until it forms a fine hair.

1 lb. shredded cocoanut, 3–4 whites beaten stiff. Add syrup to beaten whites and beat thoroughly, then stir in enough cocoanut to make the mixture very stiff. Mold into balls about an inch thick. Bake on a buttered pan in a hot oven until a light brown.

Dorothy Wade

SPONGE CAKE

To two well beaten eggs, add 1 cup of sugar, ½ cup sifted flour. Stir well, then add another ½ cup of flour with a teaspoonful of baking powder. Just before putting in pan, add ½ cup of boiling water and flavor.

Ione Holmes

WASHINGTON PIE

  • 1–3 cup butter
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 2 eggs
  • ½ cup milk
  • 1¾ cups flour
  • 2½ teaspoons baking powder

Mix as one egg cake. Bake in round layer cake pans. Put jam or jelly between layers and sprinkle top with powdered sugar.

CHRISTMAS HONEY CAKE

  • 1½ cups citron and almond nuts chopped fine
  • 2 cups brown sugar
  • 3 eggs
  • 3 cups flour
  • 2 tablespoons honey
  • 1½ teaspoons soda
  • 1 teaspoon cloves
  • ½ teaspoon allspice
  • ½ teaspoon cinnamon
  • ½ teaspoon ginger
  • ¼ teaspoon nutmeg

Beat yolks and whites of eggs separately. Add sugar to the yolks, then add honey, flour, and the soda dissolved in a little hot water. Fold in the whites of eggs and add spices and nuts. Bake in sheets and cut in triangles. Ice with thin frosting made of sugar and water.

Mamie Cameron

ONE-EGG CAKE

  • 1 cup sugar
  • 1 large tablespoon butter
  • 1 egg
  • 1½ cups flour
  • ½ cup sweet milk
  • 2 teaspoonfuls baking powder
  • 1 teaspoonful vanilla

Cream the butter and sugar. Add beaten egg, mix in milk and flour slowly.

Catherine Baxter

NUT LOAF

  • 1½ cups white flour
  • 1½ cups graham flour
  • ½ cup molasses
  • Pinch of salt
  • ¾ cup nut meats
  • 2 teaspoonfuls baking powder

Sweet milk to make soft but not thin. Let stand for ½ hour. Bake slowly in bread pan from ½ to ¾ hour.

SOUR CREAM COOKIES

Mix together 2 cups sugar, ½ cup butter, 2 eggs well beaten, ½ cup sour milk, ½ teaspoonful cinnamon and ½ teaspoonful cloves and soda. Drop by spoonfuls on buttered tins.

WALNUT CHOCOLATES

  • Whites of 3 eggs
  • ¾ cup granulated sugar
  • ¾ cup Baker’s grated chocolate
  • 1 cup chopped walnut

Beat eggs until very stiff. Add sugar gradually; then chocolate and nuts. Drop in small teaspoonfuls on buttered paper. Bake in a moderate oven.

Martha Davis

RICH CHOCOLATE CAKE

  • 1 ¾ cups sugar
  • 15 tablespoons butter
  • 3 eggs
  • 1 ½ cups flour
  • 2 teaspoons baking powder
  • ¼ lb. chocolate
  • 1 tablespoons cocoa
  • 1 teaspoon vanilla

Cream butter, add sugar gradually–yolks of eggs beaten light, melt chocolate and cocoa, milk and flour (sifted with baking powder alternately) —whites of eggs beaten stiff and vanilla. Bake one hour in moderate oven.

Miss E. I. Raitt

ROCKS

  • ½ c. brown sugar
  • 1 c. butter
  • 3 eggs beaten separately
  • 1 ½ c. raisins chopped fine
  • 2 ½ c. flour
  • 1 tsp. soda, dissolved in hot water
  • 1 tsp. cinnamon
  • 1 tsp. Vanilla

Drop from spoon into buttered pans. This will make fifty cakes.

WAFERS

  • ½ cup butter
  • 1 cup powdered sugar
  • ½ cup milk
  • 1 ¾ cups flour
  • 1 teaspoonful vanilla
  • ½ teaspoonful salt

Cream butter, sugar, add other ingredients, spread on an inverted buttered pan or baking sheet and cut while warm.

SPICE CAKES (With Dates and Nuts)

  • 1 ½ cups flour
  • 1 cup dates, cut up
  • 1 cup boiling water
  • Scant teaspoonful soda
  • 1 cup sugar
  • 1 egg
  • ½ cup butter
  • 1 teaspoonful cinnamon
  • ½ teaspoonful cloves
  • 1 cup chopped nuts

Mix dates, water and soda together.

AID SOCIETY CAKES

Put into a sauce pan the following:

  • 1 cup brown sugar
  • 1 cup water
  • 1 or 2 cups seeded raisins
  • 1-3 cup lard
  • ¼ grated nutmeg
  • 1 teaspoonful ground cloves
  • 1 teaspoonful cinnamon
  • Pinch of salt

Boil together 3 minutes; then cool. When cold add:

  • 1 teaspoonful soda dissolved in hot water
  • 2 cups flour in which ½ teaspoon baking powder has been sifted.

A few chopped nuts may be added if desired. Bake in a moderate oven.

GINGERBREAD DROPS

Beat two cupfuls of brown sugar with four eggs for 10 minutes; add two-third cup water, one pint flour two teaspoons baking powder, one tablespoon ginger, one teaspoon lemon; mix into smooth spong and bake in gem pans.

LADY BALTIMORE

Rub a cupful butter to a cream with two cups powdered sugar, add a cupful milk, when well mixed stir in the juice of one lemon and whip very light, then stir in alternately the stiffened whites of six eggs and four even cupfuls flour, sifted twice, with a round tsp. baking powder.

Mrs. H. Landes.

ANGEL FOOD CAKE

  • Whites of 11 eggs
  • 1 glass flour
  • 1 teaspoonful vanilla
  • 1 ½ glasses sugar
  • 1 teaspoonful cream of tartar

Beat whites of eggs with a little salt, until stiff and dry. Sift flour and sugar three times, then add cream of tartar and sift with flour and sugar four times. Add this to the beaten whites of eggs, stirring as little as possible. Put in ungreased cake pan, and cook 40 minutes in a slow oven. Turn the pan upside down when the cake is down, and let it remain until cold.

Cassie Lawrence

BURNED SUGAR CAKE

½ c. sugar, ½ c. butter mashed to a cream, yolks of 2 eggs well beaten, 1 cup water, 2 ½ c. flour, 2 tsp. Baking pow-der, 1.2 cup burned sugar syrup, whites of 2 eggs, beaten stiff. Bake in layers. For the sugar syrup, take 1-3 c. sugar and scorch until brown, 1-3 c. boiling water, and use ½ in the cake dough while syrup is hot, beat well. Icing—1 c. sugar mixed with ½ of burned sugar, 5 tbsp. Water, boil until it threads, pour over well beaten white of 1 egg and whip well.

Isabella Austin.

Notes

  1. The University of Washington Young Women’s Christian Association (1895-1996) was founded in 1895 by Ella Chamberlain, the head of the UW English department. It was the first women's organization on campus, and initially served as a religious organization that offered Bible study, devotional gatherings, and missionary work, including teaching English to Japanese immigrants in the 1910s. Over the years, the organization evolved from a religious group to a feminist organization that is committed to women’s health, rights, employment, racial justice, LGBTQ+ support, and freedom of speech. See YWCA records.
  2. Our text focuses on the "Dainty Desserts and Cakes" section of Kollege Kookery Kinks, which often features recipes that require baking. In the 1920s, electric ovens became increasingly common and revolutionized home baking by making it more accessible and efficient. Unlike traditional wood or coal-fired ovens, electric ovens offered precise temperature control and consistent heat distribution. Today’s readers of this cookbook can easily follow the baking instructions using a modern electric oven. See Grove.
  3. MRS. J. ALLEN SMITH: The wife of J. Allen Smith. J. Allen Smith (1860–1924) was an important figure in the early development of the University of Washington's political science and economics programs, Smith Hall was named in his honor. It was difficult to find information by searching the contributor’s name, but the example of Mrs. J. Allen Smith shows that both students and wives of faculty members contributed to this cookbook. See Arts & Sciences Web Team.
  4. Best Marshmallows= Marshmallow
  5. Milk Cracker = Cracker
  6. Nut Meat = Nut
  7. Soda = Baking Soda
  8. Sweet Milk = Milk
  9. Today, however, we would typically use a freezer to chill the mousse.
  10. The receipts instruct stirring or beating eggs or cream by hand, but today we could use electric mixers to mix these ingredients.
