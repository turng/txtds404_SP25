A Digital Edition of Bleak House and its Advertisements

Original text by: Charles Dickens

Transcription, encoding, and metadata collection by: Aries Chen, Jessica Nero, and Luke VanHouten.

Edition of the project: First draft of the full XML.

Encoded in Oxygen, distributed via Github. For educational, non-commercial use only.

The purpose of this edition is to track the differences in publishing from a serial format to a more standard book-form novel. With serial publication, the texts we know today were separated into small periodicals and chapters, only publishing a small section of the final text monthly over a large time span. Once the serial publication had finished, advertisements began getting published for the full text edition of the novel, such as in the final periodical of Bleak House. With this edition, we aim to display the functional differences in publication of different mediums of text, particularly focusing on the publication elements of the texts individually as well as the advertisements used in the periodicals and how these additions affected readership and the reader experience.

Our digital edition focuses on Charles Dickens’ Bleak House, specifically the last volume of the 1852 serialized edition and the 1853 complete first edition. Dickens wrote the book in twenty installments throughout 1852 and 1853, with the last, 19th published serial containing the 19th and 20th installments combined. Each serial publication has several pages of advertisements in the front matter of the text, which helped fund Dickens’ efforts. In particular, one of the advertisements found in the final serialized volume advertises the forthcoming first edition, and contains information regarding the copyright and printing information of that edition. A fair share of this advertising can also be found in the first novel edition, but not all of it.

The focus of our edition is to compare the publication and copyright information shared between the serialized editions (especially the final one that advertises the complete edition) and the complete first edition of the book. The advertisement in the serial provides a great deal more information regarding the timeline, setting, and cost of the printing and publication of the first edition than what the complete book provides in its own copyright pages. Readers should come away from our edition with a better understanding of how the publication process differs between serials and complete volumes, as well as how the documentation of these efforts was less robust and more scattered across different sources than with today’s methods (such as detailed copyright pages).

We have created a diplomatic transcription for our edition, and we are using it to try generate a digital facsimile, recreating exactly what is shown on the key pages of the serial and complete editions of Bleak House we are transcribing. We will not be making editorial corrrections to any of the text, although there is not too much text itself given the relative sparseness of the pages we have chosen to transcribe.

This edition has been prepared for publication as HTML through an XSLT transformation. With our focus on visual replication, this means that in encoding, we have prioritized practical considerations related to XSL and CSS over robust markup of semantic and contextual information in the base XML.

Bleak House.

Chapter I.

In Chancery.

London. Michaelmas term lately over, and the Lord Chancellor sitting in Lincoln's Inn Hall. Implacable November weather. As much mud in the streets, as if the waters had but newly retired from the face of the earth, and it would not be wonderful to meet a Megalosaurus, forty feet long or so, waddling like an elephantine lizard up Holborn-hill. Smoke lowering down from chimney-pots, making a soft black drizzle, with flakes of soot in it as big as full-grown snow-flakes—gone into mourning, one might imagine, for the death of the sun. Dogs, undistinguishable in mire. Horses, scarcely better; splashed to their very blinkers. Foot passengers, jostling one another's umbrellas, in a general infection of ill-temper, and losing their foot-hold at street-corners, where tens of thousands of other foot passengers have been slipping and sliding since the day broke (if the day ever broke), adding new deposits to the crust upon crust of mud, sticking at those points tenaciously to the pavement, and accumulating at compound interest.

Chapter LX.

Perspective.

I proceed to other passages of my narrative. From the goodness of all about me, I derived such consolation as I can never think of unmoved. I have already said so much of myself, and so much still remains, that I will not dwell upon my sorrow. I had an illness, but it was not a long one; and I would avoid even this mention of it, if I could quite keep down the recollection of their sympathy.
