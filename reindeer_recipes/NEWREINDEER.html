Reindeer Recipes

Introduction

This text is a Reindeer Recipe cookbook that was created by the Great Northern Dining Car Department in the early 20th century. The Great Northern Dining Car Department is a part of the Great Northern Railway company, which was an American Class I railroad that was created in the 19th century by James J. Hill, a Canadian-American rail entrepreneur. This rail ran from Saint Paul, Minnesota to Seattle, Washington, and was wildly successful for a majority of its existence. But since 1970, it has undergone mergers and acquisitions and has essentially been erased, or it’s at least not as visible as it once was.

That’s why we think this text is so important. It’s so far from common thought, why would you ever be thinking about the Great Northern Railroad’s Reindeer Recipe from a century ago? Even though it’s in our archives, even though we can access it with an appointment and a librarian, we still thought there was value in republishing it, in breathing some new life into it. We just made it go that much farther from being forgotten. Now we talk about it with our friends at dinner, how we’re working on a reindeer recipe cookbook, now we research the Great Northern Railroad, we read about the origins and progression of the entire company. It’s these little, personal things that keep texts alive, it doesn’t have to be on a widespread level.

Preface

A NEW discovery from Alaska—nourishment—not gold—something to add to the enjoyment of life—something that with a final analysis is found more precious than gold—something to delight the inner man—Reindeer meat.

The Great Northern Dining Car Department, ever on the alert to discover new and better foods to offer its patrons, believes that Reindeer meat will be approved by all who crave something different.

The Reindeer, as now raised in Alaska, is a domesticated animal; has nothing of the wild animal taste—the meat is finer in texture than beef and decidedly more tender—this is explained by the fact that the Reindeer is a docile animal, seldom exerting itself to the extent of providing ordinary exercise—therefore he grows fat and soft. Reindeer meat has juiciness of beef with delicacy of flavor unequalled by beef or venison. Reindeer meat is a happy medium between wild game and domestic meats.

Reindeer meat is Alaska's latest and most promising contribution to its mother country, and Great Northern Dining cars are happy in their ro le of introducing it. We confidently predict a hearty reception.

Order from the menu prepared as per one or more of the recipes mentioned in this booklet.

1. Recipes

1.1. Reindeer Hunter Soup

Cut two pounds of Reindeer meat in small pieces and put it into the soup pot (casserole), add two quarts of water. Chop one onion, a pinch of mace and one bay leaf, add to the meat, let it simmer until the meat is tender, add one cup of rice, salt and pepper and let it cook until the rice is done. Pour into the tureen over French croutons and serve.

1.2. Reindeer Stew Creole Style

Cut two pounds of meat into two-inch square pieces, salt and pepper well. Chop a couple onions very fine, brown them in a pan in a little butter, then add the meat, let it brown slightly and add a little flour. Let this brown a little. Chop a piece of cold ham very fine, then add a few cloves and a few garlic, a sprig of thyme, a sprig of parsley and one bay leaf, all minced fine. Pour soup stock or water over it, let it cook for thirty minutes, add fresh mushrooms and cook for another twenty minutes. Serve very hot.

1.3. Ragout of Reindeer

Take the breast and the neck, cut it up into neat pieces, put it into a jar, cut up some carrots, onions and celery, a few bay leaves, whole cloves, whole allspice, whole pepper, put all together in vinegar, let it stand for two or three days, then take it out and braise it to a light brown color, sprinkle it with flour and some stock, tomato juice. Let it simmer until done, season it with salt, paprika, pepper, thyme, a little chopped garlic, chopped parsley, a little sugar, tomato catsup, Worcestershire sauce, finish it with a little sour cream.

1.4. Roast Leg of Reindeer Meat

Wipe meat, sprinkle with salt and pepper, place on rack in dripping pan and dredge meat and bottom of pan with flour. Place in not too hot oven and baste as soon as flour in pan is brown, and every fifteen minutes thereafter, until meat is done, which should be cooked rare. It may be necessary to put a small quantity of water in pan while meat is cooking.

1.5. Reindeer Stew

Take a piece of the a piece of preserved venison, place in kettle, add some of the spices in which the meat has been lying, also a a piece of butter, cover well and stew until tender. When ready to serve add a generous portion of sweet cream thickened with browned flour and a a little sugar.

Cuts from the flank, breast, neck or ribs are best suited for this dish. Cut meat in pieces desired, fry in butter and bacon fat to a rich golden-brown, add boiling water and cover. After a few minutes remove scum and add the following: grated bread or flour browned in butter, grated rind of a lemon, pepper, cloves, few bay leaves, liberal quantity of cubed onions, several cucumbers cut lengthwise, and vinegar. When nearly done, add a very little apple jam or sugar to lessen sharpness of vinegar.

The sauce in which meat is served must be of generous creamy consistency and generous in quantity.

1.6. Reindeer Chops or Steaks

Chops or steaks should be broiled, or fried, on a quick fire, well seasoned and served with jelly.

1.7. Reindeer Mulligan—Hunter Style

Note: This recipe was furnished by an old hunter.

Take neck and shanks and parts of shoulder, cut in uniform pieces about two inches square, place in large wide kettle, cover with water and let simmer about half hour, add equal parts rutabagas, cut in uniform pieces about two inches square, fifteen minutes later add carrots and potatoes cut the same way. Vegetables should be equal parts, and the three together the same quantity as meat, also add several large onions, cut in quarters, then season with salt and whole black peppers; place peppers in small cheese-cloth bag and a few bay leaves, let simmer until done. Serve with dill pickles or sour pickles, according to taste.

1.8. Broiled Reindeer Meat Steak

Wipe steak, sprinkle with salt and pepper, place on greased broiler and broil five minutes. Remove to hot platter and pour over Chestnut sauce or Maitre d'Hotel butter.

1.9. Roast Loin of Reindeer Meat

Meat should be well skinned, neatly larded and pan closely covered during first part of roasting, as this will help retain juices and also insure a milder flavor. Heat liberal quantity of cubed bacon in roasting pan, add piece of butter, place loin roast in pan, larded side up and cook, well covered, for one hour. During this time and afterwards, baste frequently and as often as necessary add half cup boiling water. But during last half hour of cooking use thick sour cream in place of water. Roast, according to size of meat, from 2–2:30 hours, without turning. Must be cooked rare. Stir up gravy with cold water and if not sufficiently creamy stiffen with some flour.

1.10. Saddle of Reindeer—Larded

Take the saddle and lard it, season it with salt and pepper, thyme, fry it on a quick fire in a sauté pan, a few spices, add some Espagnole, a little jelly, lemon juice. Do not cook it too much—it should be medium cooked. When done finish the sauce with a little chopped garlic, chopped parsley and a little cream and nutmeg.

Appendix A Notes

Note: We decided to keep this beginning part, even though  the tone is a bit precarious (calling Reindeer), as it set a  lighthearted and slightly innocent air for this cookbook
Note: Vague instructions like this are preserved to  try to maintain the contextual integrity of the cookbook, in this  case the very rustic and independent sentiment of figuring it out  yourself
Note: We thought the recipes like these, the ones where the title is  basically the recipe, were very helpful in establishing the intent  of the cookbook—that is, doing minimal tweaks on an otherwise  rudimentary food source.
Note: James J. Hill: He was born in Canada on September 16, 1838 and died in Minnesota on May 29, 1916. He was a rail entrepreneur responsible for the entirety of the Great Northern Railroad and he amassed a wealth of $63 million by the time he died.
Note: Reindeer: Reindeer are indiginous to subarctic regions, much like some parts of North America. With this being said, the nearby areas of the rail itself would’ve been close to reindeer populations, making reindeer an extremely viable source of food, hence the entire cookbook.
Note: Great Northern Railroad: Now known as the Burlington Northern and Santa Fe Railway.
Note: For the purposes of modern readability, we decided to convert the vague-er measurements to more conventional measurements in the encoding process
